# Truth and Edge

This lens combines demand reality with strategic shape.

## Truth Questions

Use these when they actually sharpen the concept:

- who feels the pain most acutely?
- what do they do today instead?
- what real behavior suggests the problem matters?
- what would count as real feedback instead of polite interest?
- what kind of product is this trying to become: startup bet, internal tool,
  craft product, research system, or incremental feature?

## Edge Questions

Use these to shape the concept strategically:

- what is the narrowest wedge that still creates value?
- what difficult part is worth embracing because it becomes the moat?
- what deliberately simple version should be tried first to learn faster?
- what observation would materially change the concept?
- what expansion path should be considered from the start?
- if the product posture warrants it, what viral or distribution surface should
  be designed in early?

## Rules

- ask one question at a time when tradeoffs are real
- push for specificity, not volume
- prefer evidence and concrete examples over abstract enthusiasm
- skip questions already answered by the repo, docs, or earlier conversation
- do not force "go bigger" or "go smaller" as dogma; use both hardness and
  simplification intentionally
- ask the product posture question before assuming startup-style growth logic
- legacy extensions or narrow internal tools may not need viral thinking at all

## Failure Patterns

- solution in search of a problem
- interest mistaken for demand
- vague users with no concrete actor
- broad ambition with no wedge
- a hard problem with no plausible edge
- a simple experiment that teaches nothing meaningful
- growth, distribution, or feedback treated as an afterthought
