# Problem Framing

`ideation` should push a concept until it is concrete enough to shape, but not
yet procedural like `spec`.

## High-Leverage Questions

Use these only when they actually sharpen the concept:

- who feels the pain most acutely?
- what do they do today instead?
- what real behavior suggests the problem matters?
- what is the narrowest wedge that still creates value?
- what observation would change the concept materially?
- what future direction does this wedge open?

These came from startup-style office-hours and CEO-review patterns, but the
portable value is the forcing function, not the founder persona.

## Rules

- ask one question at a time when tradeoffs are real
- push for specificity, not volume
- prefer evidence and concrete examples over abstract enthusiasm
- if the user already answered a later question, skip it

## Failure Patterns

Watch for:

- solution in search of a problem
- interest mistaken for demand
- vague users with no concrete actor
- broad ambition with no wedge
- design energy spent on the wrong abstraction level
